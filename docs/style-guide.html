01

Brand at a Glance

Dan Tremblay Music sits at the intersection of two credible things: thirty years of award‑winning piano instruction, and an active career composing and producing music licensed worldwide. Every piece of content — a lesson page, a newsletter — should read as both at once: a teacher parents trust, and a working musician whose ear you'd trust too.

That's the whole brief. Nothing below exists to make things prettier for its own sake — it exists so a newsletter put together in a hurry still looks like it belongs next to the site.

The score becomes the sound.

02

Mark & Monogram

The site already has a mark: a serif D + T monogram, rendered white, used in the nav bar, the footer, and as the favicon. Nothing here proposes a new one — every application below reuses this exact file, /images/dt-white.png, as-is.

Dan Tremblay
MUSIC

As it already appears on the live site — reused here, not redrawn.

Using it: it's a white-only asset, so it only works on a dark ground — navy or brand-blue, exactly as it already sits in the nav and footer. It won't read directly on the light canvas without a dark chip behind it, the way it's placed in the sidebar and email examples below. Don't recolor it, redraw it, or stretch it off its square proportion.

03

Color

Four colors, no exceptions — the same set already live on the site. Thumbnails and social platforms reward loud color; the restraint is the differentiator.

05

Voice & Tone

Specific and credential-led, the way the site already talks — never generic edtech uplift. If a line could sit on any music school's page, rewrite it with a real number, name, or result.

Use"8× Provincial Piano Awards"
Avoid"Unlock your musical potential!"
Use"Thirty years at the keys."
Avoid"Join us on a musical journey."
Use"Mixed and mastered for [Artist]."
Avoid"Amazing new track alert!! 🔥🔥"
06

Email Newsletters

Headers stay on the canvas color, not navy — dark bands render inconsistently across mail clients. The call-to-action button is the site's existing .btn-filled spec exactly, so a reader who clicks through lands somewhere that already looks familiar.
